# Viability: south-facing sloped site with north berm

**Status:** In progress
**Verdict:** Go with changes (working assumption — **Rapid City / Black Hills, SD**, 44.08°N)

## Question

Should this house sit on a gentle south-facing slope, with the north (rear) wall partially bermed into the grade, and the south (glazed) wall on the low/downhill side?

## Constraints

- Footprint 60' (E–W) × 25' (N–S), long south glass wall must stay fully sun-exposed
- Slope should be **shallow** — north wall only *partially* underground, not a cut-in hillside
- Bermed/below-grade north wall needs waterproofing, drainage, and lateral earth-load structure
- South (downhill) side must shed water away from the building, not toward it
- Passive-solar overhang geometry driven by **44.08°N** design latitude (see [03-passive-solar](../03-passive-solar/viability.md)), not by site slope

## Options

1. **Flat site** — simplest structurally; no berm benefit; more exposed north wall
2. **Gentle south slope + partial north berm** *(chosen working assumption)* — thermal buffer + wind protection on cold side, south stays open
3. **Steeper slope / full walkout basement** — more usable below-grade volume but bigger retaining/waterproofing scope and cost
4. **Berm built up artificially on flat land** — get berm benefit without a natural slope; earthwork cost

## Analysis

*Rough geometry to pin down:*

- Over a 25' N–S depth, a **2% slope** drops ~0.5', **5%** ~1.25', **10%** ~2.5'. A partial berm of ~3–5' at the north wall implies either a modestly steeper natural grade (~12–20% across the footprint) or supplemental earthwork/step in the slab.
- Decide whether finish floor is a single level (north wall does the retaining) or a slight step/split follows grade.
- Below-grade north wall = insulated retaining assembly (drainage board, perimeter drain, dampproofing). Ties into [06-structure](../06-structure/) and [02-envelope](../02-envelope/).
